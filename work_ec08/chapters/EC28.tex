\begin{multicols}{2}
\begin{engineeringnews}
Quantum technologies are moving from laboratory demonstrations to specialised sensors, secure communication links and prototype computers. Their potential comes from preparing, controlling and measuring individual quantum states rather than merely reducing the size of conventional devices.
\end{engineeringnews}

\begin{ecarticle}
A classical bit takes the value 0 or 1. A qubit can be prepared in a superposition $\alpha\lvert 0\rangle+\beta\lvert 1\rangle$, where the complex amplitudes satisfy $|\alpha|^2+|\beta|^2=1$. A measurement returns either 0 or 1 with probabilities determined by these amplitudes. Several qubits may also become entangled, so their measurement results cannot be described as independent classical variables.

A quantum computer does not simply test every possible answer at once. Quantum gates modify probability amplitudes, while interference suppresses some outcomes and reinforces others. The final measurement produces an ordinary bit string, so a useful algorithm must concentrate probability on informative results before measurement. Quantum algorithms can accelerate selected tasks such as factoring, quantum simulation or particular search and optimisation subroutines, but they do not make every computation faster.

The main engineering challenge is decoherence. Unwanted interactions with the environment disturb phase relationships and progressively destroy the stored quantum information. Superconducting circuits may require millikelvin temperatures and carefully filtered microwave signals. Trapped ions and neutral atoms need ultra-high vacuum, stable lasers and precise electromagnetic fields. Photonic systems avoid some cooling constraints but require low-loss sources, detectors and optical circuits.

Quantum error correction encodes one logical qubit in many physical qubits. Repeated syndrome measurements reveal certain errors without directly reading the logical state. This can support fault-tolerant computation, but only when physical error rates remain below a demanding threshold and when enough hardware is available. The number of physical qubits required for one reliable logical qubit can therefore be very large.

Quantum sensing is closer to practical deployment in several fields. Atomic clocks use stable transition frequencies, while magnetometers and gravimeters detect tiny variations in magnetic field or acceleration. Sensitivity often improves with longer interrogation time, but longer measurements also allow more decoherence and environmental drift. Engineers must therefore optimise preparation time, coherence, repetition rate and signal processing.

Quantum communication can reveal interception because measuring an unknown quantum state generally disturbs it. However, a complete secure system still depends on trusted hardware, authentication, classical cryptography and careful implementation. Real engineering performance must be demonstrated through repeatable benchmarks, calibration, standards and integration with conventional equipment.
\end{ecarticle}

\begin{engineeringchallenge}
A qubit has an initial coherence $C_0=1$ and a coherence time $T_2=80\,\mu\mathrm{s}$. Assume $C(t)=\exp(-t/T_2)$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item Calculate the remaining coherence after $40\,\mu\mathrm{s}$ and $120\,\mu\mathrm{s}$.
\item Determine the maximum operation time required to keep $C(t)\geq0.70$.
\item A quantum gate lasts $50\,\mathrm{ns}$. Estimate how many sequential gates fit within this time limit.
\item Explain why this number is only an upper bound in a real processor.
\end{enumerate}
\end{engineeringchallenge}
